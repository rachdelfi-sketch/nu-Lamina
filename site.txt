    <section id="what-is">
      <h2>What is Nu-L?</h2>
      <p>
        Nu-L is a new kind of logic device. It is not a classic transistor. Instead,
        it is a vertical field-summation element built on a thin substrate:
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>Multiple independent gate electrodes on one side</li>
        <li>A continuous semiconductor channel on the other side</li>
        <li>An ultrathin dielectric window between them</li>
        <li>A vertical via carrying the output signal</li>
      </ul>
      <p>
        Logic is performed by the weighted sum of electric fields from all gates,
        rather than by connecting many transistors in series and parallel.
      </p>
    </section>

    <section id="why-it-matters">
      <h2>Why It Matters</h2>
      <p><strong>Conventional CMOS logic:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Uses multiple transistors to implement each logic function</li>
        <li>Requires advanced, expensive lithography and fabs</li>
        <li>Is reaching practical and economic scaling limits</li>
      </ul>
      <p><strong>Nu-L approach:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Implements functions (XOR, NAND, majority) in a single vertical device</li>
        <li>Works with thin-film, printed, and hybrid processes</li>
        <li>Is designed for modular, stackable compute sheets</li>
      </ul>
    </section>

    <section id="how-it-works">
      <h2>How the Nu-L Device Works</h2>
      <p>
        Each gate input sets a voltage on one of the gate electrodes. These gates
        generate electric fields that penetrate the ultrathin dielectric and act on
        the semiconductor channel on the opposite side.
      </p>
      <p>
        The channel “feels” the weighted sum of all gate fields. If the combined
        field exceeds a threshold, the channel turns on and drives the vertical via.
        A single Nu-L cell can therefore behave like:
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>XOR or XNOR</li>
        <li>NAND or NOR</li>
        <li>Majority or threshold logic</li>
      </ul>
    </section>

    <section id="sheet-arch">
      <h2>Sheet Architecture</h2>
      <p>
        Nu-L devices are integrated into thin sheets with three main zones:
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Sealed Nu-L core:</strong> arrays of Nu-L devices, no lateral routing.</li>
        <li><strong>External logic zones:</strong> printed logic, memory, clock and IO circuitry.</li>
        <li><strong>Docking edge:</strong> standardized connector to link sheets together.</li>
      </ul>
      <p>
        Multiple sheets can be stacked, forming a modular three-dimensional compute
        assembly naturally cooled by airflow between layers.
      </p>
    </section>

    <section id="applications">
